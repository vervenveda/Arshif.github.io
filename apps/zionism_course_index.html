Mini-Course:
Origins of Israel and Zionism

Introduction

This module provides a neutral overview of the origins of Zionism, the British Mandate in Palestine, and the establishment of the State of Israel in 1948. All information is based on historical records and widely documented sources.

Key Facts

What is Zionism?
Zionism emerged in late-19th-century Europe as a political and cultural movement advocating for the establishment of a Jewish homeland. It was a response to widespread antisemitism and persecution.
British Mandate in Palestine
After World War I, Britain administered Palestine under a League of Nations mandate from 1920 to 1948. British policies influenced migration, land ownership, and tensions between Jewish and Arab communities.
United Nations Partition Plan
In 1947, the UN proposed partitioning Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem under international administration. The plan was accepted by Jewish leaders and rejected by Arab leaders.
Declaration of the State of Israel
On May 14, 1948, Jewish leaders declared the independence of Israel. This followed decades of Zionist organization, immigration, and political lobbying.

Timeline of Key Events

1897 - First Zionist Congress

The First Zionist Congress convened in Basel, Switzerland, marking the formal beginning of the Zionist movement.

1917 - Balfour Declaration

Britain expressed support for "a national home for the Jewish people" in Palestine, while pledging to respect the rights of existing communities.

1920-1948 - British Mandate

Britain administered Palestine, managing immigration, land policies, and communal tensions between Jewish and Arab populations.

1947 - UN Partition Plan

The UN proposed dividing Palestine into Jewish and Arab states. Jewish leadership accepted, Arab leadership rejected.

1948 - Declaration of Independence

The State of Israel was declared on May 14, 1948, followed by conflict with neighboring Arab states.

Reflection

Consider how ideology, colonial administration, and international decisions intersected in the establishment of Israel. Think critically about the historical causes and consequences, without attributing all outcomes to a single factor.
